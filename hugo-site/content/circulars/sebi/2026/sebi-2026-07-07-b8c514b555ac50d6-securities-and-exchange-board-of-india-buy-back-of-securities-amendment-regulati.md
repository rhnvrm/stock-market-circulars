## Summary

SEBI has notified the Securities and Exchange Board of India (Buy-Back of Securities) (Amendment) Regulations, 2026, amending the SEBI (Buy-Back of Securities) Regulations, 2018. The amendment was published in the Gazette of India on July 1, 2026 (Notification No. SEBI/LAD-NRO/GN/2026/306) and will come into force on August 1, 2026. Key changes include a new cap limiting open market buybacks conducted through stock exchanges to less than 15% of a company's paid-up capital and free reserves (on both standalone and consolidated basis), a revised cooling-off period before a company can launch a new buyback offer, and a new restriction preventing buybacks that would cause a company to fall below minimum public shareholding requirements.

## Key Points

- Amendment to Regulation 4 extends an existing transitional date reference from "April 1, 2025" to "July 31, 2026".
- A new third proviso to Regulation 4(iv)(b) caps open market (stock exchange route) buybacks: from August 1, 2026, the buyback amount must be less than 15% of the company's paid-up capital and free reserves, assessed on both standalone and consolidated financial statement basis.
- Sub-regulation 4(vii) is replaced: a company that has previously made a buyback offer cannot make a fresh buyback offer until the cooling-off period prescribed under the Companies Act, 2013 has elapsed from the expiry of the previous offer.
- New sub-regulation 4(xi) added: a company shall not make a buyback offer of shares or specified securities that would result in a breach of minimum public shareholding requirements under the Securities Contracts (Regulation) Rules, 1957 or the SEBI (Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations, 2015.
- Regulation 8(i) has minor wording/drafting changes (terminology substitution) with no substantive change in intent noted in the extracted text.

## Regulatory Changes

The amendment modifies the SEBI (Buy-Back of Securities) Regulations, 2018 as follows:

1. **Regulation 4(iv)(b)**: Textual and cross-reference edits, and extension of the transitional relief period from "1 April 2025" to "31 July 2026".
2. **New third proviso to Regulation 4(iv)(b)**: Effective August 1, 2026, any buyback carried out through the open market via stock exchanges must not equal or exceed 15% of the company's paid-up capital and free reserves, computed on both a standalone and consolidated basis.
3. **Regulation 4(vii)** (substituted): Introduces a mandatory cooling-off period, aligned with the Companies Act, 2013, before a company can launch another buyback offer after a prior one has lapsed.
4. **New Regulation 4(xi)**: Prohibits buyback offers that would cause non-compliance with minimum public shareholding norms under SCRR, 1957 or SEBI LODR Regulations, 2015.
5. **Regulation 8(i)**: Minor drafting/terminology amendment.

## Compliance Requirements

- Listed companies planning buybacks via the open market/stock exchange route must ensure the buyback size remains below 15% of paid-up capital and free reserves (standalone and consolidated) once the amendment takes effect.
- Companies must track the cooling-off period under the Companies Act, 2013 before initiating a subsequent buyback offer after a previous one has expired.
- Boards and compliance teams must verify that any proposed buyback will not push public shareholding below the minimum thresholds mandated by SCRR, 1957 and SEBI LODR Regulations, 2015 before proceeding.
- Companies and their merchant bankers/compliance officers should review buyback documentation and board resolutions in light of the amended thresholds ahead of the effective date.

## Important Dates

- **July 1, 2026**: Notification published in the Gazette of India (Extraordinary), Part III, Section 4.
- **August 1, 2026**: Effective date of the amended regulations, including the new 15% open-market buyback cap and minimum public shareholding safeguard.
- **July 31, 2026**: Revised end date of the transitional provision referenced in amended Regulation 4(iv)(b).

## Impact Assessment

The amendment materially tightens the framework for open-market buybacks by introducing a quantitative ceiling (15% of paid-up capital and free reserves) that did not previously exist in this form, likely constraining the size of future open-market buyback programs for many listed companies. The cooling-off period revision and the explicit bar on buybacks that would breach minimum public shareholding rules add further compliance checkpoints for corporate treasury and legal teams. Companies with buybacks in the pipeline around the August 1, 2026 effective date should reassess offer sizing and timing to ensure compliance. The changes are procedural/compliance-oriented rather than immediately market-moving, but could affect capital allocation strategy for companies that rely heavily on open-market buybacks for shareholder returns.
