## Summary

The 214th SEBI Board Meeting held on June 19, 2026 in Mumbai approved two major regulatory reforms: (1) a comprehensive overhaul of the securities transmission framework to ease the process for legal heirs of deceased investors, and (2) re-introduction of open market buyback through stock exchanges, effective August 1, 2026, along with broader amendments to the SEBI (Buy-back of Securities) Regulations, 2018.

## Key Points

- New Quick Transmission Processing (QTP) category introduced for small-value claims: up to ₹10,000 for physical holdings and up to ₹30,000 for dematerialised holdings.
- Documentation limits for simplified transmission doubled — from ₹5 lakh to ₹10 lakh for physical holdings (per listed company) and from ₹15 lakh to ₹30 lakh for dematerialised holdings (per beneficial owner).
- Open market buyback through stock exchanges re-introduced with effect from August 1, 2026, providing companies an additional buyback route.
- Open market buybacks must be completed within 66 working days from opening, with at least 40% of earmarked funds utilised in the first half of the buyback period.
- Shareholders to receive buyback information via electronic means in addition to existing newspaper public announcements.

## Regulatory Changes

### 1. Transmission of Securities Framework
- **PAN submission removed**: PAN is already on record for demat accounts, so re-submission is no longer required.
- **Probate of Will no longer mandatory**: Aligned with recent amendments to succession laws.
- **Combined Affidavit-cum-NOC permitted**: Replaces the earlier requirement for separate affidavit and NOC documents.
- **QR Code death certificates accepted**: Copies of death certificates bearing QR codes are now eligible documents, easing verification.
- **Foreign death certificates**: Additional verification modes specified — overseas branches of Indian banks or foreign banks with correspondent banking relationships with Indian banks.

### 2. Open Market Buyback – SEBI (Buy-back of Securities) Regulations, 2018
- Open market buyback through stock exchanges re-introduced effective **August 1, 2026**, supplementing the existing tender offer and book-building routes.
- Dissemination of buyback information to shareholders mandated through electronic means alongside newspaper advertisements.
- Buyback window capped at **66 working days**; minimum **40% of earmarked funds** must be deployed in the first half of the period.
- Promoters are not permitted to participate in open market buybacks (per revised taxation and regulatory framework).

## Compliance Requirements

- **Listed companies** planning buybacks should note the new open market route and the 66-working-day completion and 40% front-loading requirements applicable from August 1, 2026.
- **Registrars and Transfer Agents (RTAs)** and **depositories** must update transmission processing systems to reflect new QTP limits, revised documentation standards, and removal of PAN/Probate requirements.
- **Mutual funds (AMCs)** should align transmission processes with the revised framework as per AMFI consultation.
- All intermediaries should update SOPs and investor-facing documentation to reflect accepted QR Code death certificates and foreign jurisdiction verification modes.

## Impact Assessment

**Transmission Framework**: These changes significantly reduce the procedural and documentation burden on legal heirs and nominees of deceased investors. The QTP category for small-value claims and the doubling of simplified-documentation thresholds will accelerate transmission timelines and reduce litigation risk. Intermediaries will need to operationalise the new standards but benefit from clearer, standardised processes.

**Open Market Buyback**: Re-introduction of the stock exchange route gives companies greater flexibility in executing buybacks, especially following changes in the taxation framework. The 66-working-day window and 40% front-loading rule provide structural discipline, protecting investors from indefinite or poorly-executed programmes. The electronic dissemination requirement improves transparency and shareholder reach. Overall, this is positive for market liquidity and corporate capital management flexibility.
