## Summary

NSE's Listing department has notified the admission of 6 new government securities to dealings on the National Stock Exchange Capital Market segment with effect from June 01, 2026. The securities comprise 3 State Development Loans (SDLs) from Gujarat, Uttarakhand, and Bihar, and 3 Government of India Treasury Bills with tenors of 91 days, 182 days, and 364 days. All securities carry a standard lot size of 100.

## Key Points

- 6 new securities admitted to NSE Capital Market segment effective June 01, 2026
- 3 SDLs: Gujarat (7.74% 2038), Uttarakhand (7.87% 2044), Bihar (7.84% 2041)
- 3 GOI T-bills: 91-day (maturing 28-Aug-2026), 182-day (maturing 27-Nov-2026), 364-day (maturing 28-May-2027)
- All securities carry a uniform lot size of 100
- Securities must be identified and traded only by their designated exchange codes
- Issued under Regulation 3.1.1 and Regulation 2.5.5 of NSE Capital Market Trading Regulations Part A

## Regulatory Changes

No new regulatory changes. This circular is issued in routine compliance with Regulation 3.1.1 (admission of securities to dealings) and Regulation 2.5.5 (lot sizes) of the NSE Capital Market Trading Regulations Part A.

## Compliance Requirements

- Members must use designated security codes for trading; no other identifier is permitted on the trading system
- Trading must be executed in the specified lot sizes (100 units per lot for all 6 securities)

## Important Dates

- **Effective Date:** June 01, 2026 — all 6 securities available for trading on NSE Capital Market segment
- **SDL Maturities:** May 27, 2038 (GJ), May 27, 2044 (UK), May 27, 2041 (BR)
- **T-bill Maturities:** August 28, 2026 (91D), November 27, 2026 (182D), May 28, 2027 (364D)

## Impact Assessment

This is a routine listing notification with low market impact. The addition of 3 SDL instruments from state governments and 3 standard-tenor GOI T-bills expands the tradeable government securities universe on NSE. SDL instruments with coupon rates of 7.74%–7.87% offer medium-to-long duration exposure (2038–2044 maturities), while the T-bills provide short-term liquidity instruments. No disruption to existing trading operations is expected.
